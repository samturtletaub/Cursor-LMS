If you are coming from cyber resilience sales, your superpower is **risk, urgency, and economic framing**. The gap is not “business acumen”—it is **how engineering work actually flows** and how technical buyers evaluate tools that touch the SDLC.

## The SDLC in seller-friendly terms

Most teams describe delivery as a loop (not a waterfall checklist):

1. **Plan** — decide what to build, why it matters, and how to slice it.
2. **Code** — implement changes in an IDE with tight feedback loops.
3. **Review** — humans (and increasingly automation) validate correctness and risk.
4. **Test** — prove the change works: unit tests, integration tests, QA, canaries.
5. **Deploy** — ship to users safely (progressive rollout, feature flags).
6. **Monitor** — observe production signals and feed learnings back into planning.

<KeyTakeaway>
Buyers rarely argue about the SDLC “theory.” They argue about **where time is lost**, **where failures are expensive**, and **what must be governed** (security, compliance, reliability).
</KeyTakeaway>

## Roles: who cares about what

You do not need to memorize every title. You need a reliable map from **role → incentives → common objections**.

- **IC engineer**: speed, focus, fewer context switches; fears tools that add friction or feel “bossware.”
- **Tech lead / Staff**: architecture risk, maintainability, review burden; fears low-quality automation.
- **Engineering manager**: team throughput, hiring/retention, predictability; fears thrash and rework.
- **Director / VP Engineering**: delivery commitments, org scalability, engineering brand; fears outages and inefficiency.
- **CTO / VP Eng (enterprise economic buyer in many strategic deals)**: platform bets, security posture, vendor sprawl, long-term leverage.
- **Security / IT**: data handling, access control, audit evidence, procurement alignment.

<Callout title="Enterprise reality">
In strategic accounts, **multiple personas co-own the decision**. Your job is to **sequence proof** so each stakeholder hears the part of the story that maps to their KPIs—without sounding like you are reading a different script in every room.
</Callout>

## A developer’s day (the “collaboration stack”)

Most IC engineers live across a small set of surfaces:

- **IDE** (where code changes start)
- **Git** + **PRs** (where change is reviewed and merged)
- **CI/CD** (where change is validated and shipped)
- **Chat** (Slack/Teams) and **tickets** (Jira/Linear) (where coordination happens)

When you sell Cursor, you are not selling “a chat window.” You are selling **less context switching** between those surfaces—especially when the work spans multiple files and deep repository context.

## Friction you should be able to name credibly

These are not the only frictions—but they show up constantly in discovery:

- **Context loading cost** (“where does this bug actually live?”)
- **Review latency** (small PRs still take time; large PRs are riskier)
- **Boilerplate + repetitive edits** (especially across services)
- **Knowledge silos** (onboarding, legacy code, poorly documented domains)

## What “good” sounds like in discovery

You are aiming for questions that reveal **workflow**, not trivia:

- “Walk me through the last time a change touched more than three files—where did the team lose time?”
- “Who owns review standards for AI-generated changes?”
- “When something breaks in prod, what’s the first place engineers look—and what would ‘fast’ look like?”
