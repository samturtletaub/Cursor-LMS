## The buying committee (typical shapes)

No two deals are identical, but enterprise software purchases often include:

- **End users / champions** (developers)
- **Engineering leadership** (VP/Director level)
- **CTO / senior technology leadership** (especially strategic accounts)
- **Security / IT** (risk, access, compliance evidence)
- **Procurement / finance** (commercial mechanics, alternatives, benchmarks)

<KeyTakeaway>
Developer tools often **start bottoms-up** and **expand tops-down**. Your value is helping the org turn grassroots usage into a governed standard without killing the organic adoption story.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Persona cheat-sheet (what to listen for)

- **Developer champion**: talks about daily friction, keyboard flow, PR cycle time.
- **EM**: talks about sprint predictability, team norms, onboarding.
- **VP Eng**: talks about platform decisions, vendor consolidation, engineering efficiency at scale.
- **CTO**: talks about architecture direction, risk posture, long-term bets.
- **Security**: talks about data flows, SSO/SCIM, logging, DLP-adjacent concerns, vendor review.
- **Procurement**: talks about price-to-value, bundling, contract flex, rollout timing.

<Callout title="Discovery discipline">
Ask questions that create **shared language** between personas. If Security and Engineering mean different things by “data residency,” your deal stalls in the hallway.
</Callout>

## Land-and-expand patterns (dev tools)

Common pattern:

1. Individual adoption within teams
2. Team standardization (shared norms, budgets consolidate)
3. Enterprise standardization (security, procurement, global rollout)

Your enablement goal is to know **what evidence moves the deal between stages**.
