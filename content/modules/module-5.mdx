## How to compete without sounding tribal

Buyers expect you to know the landscape. The tone that wins in strategic accounts is **curious, specific, and evidence-seeking**:

- “What workflow is the team optimizing for?”
- “What did the last evaluation measure—speed, quality, security controls, or adoption?”
- “Who decided ‘good enough’—engineering leadership, security, or procurement?”

<KeyTakeaway>
Your differentiation should be anchored in **workflow depth**, **enterprise controls**, and **how evaluations are won in practice**—not a meme-tier feature checklist.
</KeyTakeaway>

## One-line anchors (starting points, not scripture)

Use these as **hypotheses** you validate in discovery:

- **GitHub Copilot**: deeply integrated GitHub workflow; bundling power; strong default for GitHub-centric shops.
- **Claude Code / terminal-native agents**: strong autonomy story for engineers who live in CLI workflows.
- **JetBrains ecosystem**: IDE-native depth for teams standardized on JetBrains.
- **Amazon Q Developer**: AWS-centric buyer alignment and packaging dynamics.
- **Windsurf / others**: category noise is high—ask what the team tried and what broke in real repos.

<Callout title="Copilot objection starter">
“We already have Copilot via GitHub.” Respond with discovery: **where multi-file work stalls**, **how review is handled for agentic changes**, **what controls are required**, and **what developers choose when they can pick**.
</Callout>

## Battle-card discipline

A great battle card is not “why we win.” It is:

- **Trigger**: when this competitor shows up
- **Landmine questions**: what to ask to expose weakness
- **Proof**: what evidence closes doubt (POC design, security artifacts, references)
