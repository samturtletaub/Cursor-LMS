## Land-and-expand (what good looks like)

Most strong dev-tool motions combine:

- **Organic usage** that proves value in real workflows
- **Team adoption** that standardizes norms and budgets
- **Enterprise governance** that makes Security/IT a partner, not a gate with a random checklist

<KeyTakeaway>
Your job is to **make expansion feel inevitable** because the org already proved value—then help them **govern it responsibly**.
</KeyTakeaway>

## ROI without embarrassing math

Strong ROI stories share a structure:

1. **Baseline cost of engineering time** (fully loaded or directional—be honest about estimates)
2. **A measurable workflow lever** (review time, rework, onboarding time, incident recovery—pick what the buyer believes)
3. **A conservative improvement hypothesis** (ranges beat false precision)
4. **Risk-adjusted implementation** (training, guardrails, evaluation design)

<Callout title="Credible seller behavior">
If a stat is promotional, treat it as a **hypothesis** until validated in the account’s context.
</Callout>

## Security & compliance conversations

Expect detailed questions on identity, access, data handling, retention, and audit evidence. Your advantage is **structured follow-up**:

- confirm claims against **primary trust/security documentation**
- involve specialists when needed
- never bluff in front of a CISO

## MEDDPICC (dev-tool translation)

- **Metrics**: engineering throughput, incident rates, onboarding time, developer satisfaction signals—pick what the buyer owns.
- **Economic buyer**: often engineering leadership; CTO enters earlier in strategic accounts.
- **Decision criteria**: includes security requirements and developer experience requirements—both matter.
- **Paper process**: procurement, legal, data processing terms, vendor risk reviews.
